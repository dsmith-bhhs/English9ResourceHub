Reader-Response Journal Prompts1
How the Author Writes
● Topic: What was his or her subject? Why this topic?
● Plot: What happened; what were the events of the story?
● Pace: How quickly or slowly did the author move the plot? Was it gradual
enough to be believable and keep you involved? Fast enough to hold a
reader’s interest? Was there too much action and not enough character
development?
● Plausibility/Believability: Did the plot ring true? Would characters act and
react this way? Were the circumstances believable? Did it matter?
● Description and detail: Could we see it happening? Feel it? Hear it? Too little
description? Too many details?
● Dialogue: Was the talk realistic? Could we hear the individual characters’
voices? Too much dialogue? Too little? What did the dialogue show about
the moods, ages, intentions, and backgrounds of the characters?
● Flashbacks, flash-forwards, and foreshadows: How did the author use shifts
in time, and why?
● Emotions: Did the reader laugh? Cry? Why?
● Setting: What time and place did the author choose? Why? Was the setting
important to the story? Was it convincing/believable? Confusing?
● Main characters: Who were they? How can you tell who the main character
is?
● Character development: How were characters introduced and developed?
How were their actions, thoughts, and feelings depicted? Were they
believable? Could you enter characters’ hearts and minds and see through
their eyes? Which characters did you relate to? Did the reader care about
what happened to any of them? Did any of them remind you of characters
from movies, plays, or other novels? Of people from the reader’s real life?
● Titles: Did it fit? Was it a grabber? Did it give too much away?
● Theme: What ideas about life and living come through the story?
● Problem: What was the issue for the main character to try to resolve?
● Suspense: Did you wonder what would happen next? How did the author
establish a suspenseful tone? Did the author surprise you?
● Formula: Could you predict too easily what was going to happen? Was it too
much like other books by this author or from this genre?

● Conventions: Did you notice short paragraphs or chapters? Sentence
fragments? Why did the author write this way?
● Information: Were there enough specifics about character, action,
background, and setting? Was there too much information? Irrelevant/Not
important information?
● Specific information: What did you learn about the world— about history,
art, politics, science, etc.—through the narrative?
● Length: Was this book too long? Too short?
● Point of view: Who told the story? What voice did the author choose: First
person? Third person? Single character, dual, multiple, or no character? Why
did the author choose this point of view? What were the advantages and
drawbacks for you? Where did you stand in relation to the narrator?
● Grace of language: Did the sentences flow? Were they choppy? Did you
savor particular phrases and sentences? Was there figurative language?
Was there imagery: did the writing create pictures in your mind? Were there
sensory details?
● First sentence: How did the author bring you in?
● Lead: How did the author try to keep you there?
● Conclusion: How did the author leave you? Why this ending?
● Prologues: How were these special introductions used and to what effect?
● Epilogues: How was this special conclusion used and to what effect?
● Unusual or experimental narrative techniques: What did you think?
● In-jokes: Did you pick up on inside jokes in the text?
The Author (for those who have read other works by the same author)
● Speculations about, or references to published accounts of, an author’s
writing processes
● Titles of other books by an author, including sequels, trilogies, and series
● Comparisons with other books by an author
● Comparisons with other authors’ styles
● Comparisons with similar books, in terms of topic, genre, or theme, by other
authors Biographical information about an author
● Publishing-world gossip about an author
● How authors use elements of their own lives and experiences in their fiction
● Other ways authors might have researched their subjects
● News of a new release by an author

Your Strategies
● Choice: How do you decide what to read?
● Place: Did you skim, skip, slow down, regress, speed up, or look ahead?
Why? When? What was the effect?
● Abandoning: How and when did you make this decision to abandon the
book?
● Rereading: Why did you reread this book? What differences are noted a
second time through?
● Revisiting particular parts of a book: Why did you skim back? For pleasure?
Clarification?

● Planning: Do you anticipate reading a particular book, author, or genre?
● Predicting: Did you imagine what would happen next? Was the guess
correct?
● Revising: Did you consider other ways an author might have written?
● Connecting: Did you relate a book to another book? To a poem or song? To
your experiences or feelings?
● Analyzing: What did you think the book was about?
● Length of time it took to read a book Why so long or so quickly?
● Reading rate: How many pages did you read in a half hour? How did you
vary your rate depending on the type of book?
● Reading “too easy” books: Why do you need mindless breaks sometimes?
● Difficulty What makes a book a challenge? What do you do when a book is
difficult? What do you do when it’s too difficult?
● Schema: Did you have adequate background experience to understand and
appreciate this book?
● Unknown Vocabulary: What did you do when you came across an unfamiliar
word?
● Contentives, eye fixations, and peripheral vision: What did you notice your
eyes doing during reading?
● Rituals: When, how, and where do you read? Why?
● How did you learn to read?
