Literary Terms
Setting: the time period, location, and circumstances of the work.
Point of View: the narrative perspective from which a story is told. This may be 1st person, 3rd
person limited, or 3rd person omniscient.
Characterization: the conveying of details about a character. This may be through
descriptions, conversations, or the character’s actions and thoughts said by the character or
about that character by others.
Direct characterization: when the author directly states descriptions of the
character’s physical appearance or personality.
Indirect characterization: when the reader has to make inferences about the
character based upon their behavior, words, or interactions with others or by what others
say or do around that character.
Theme: the author’s purpose or underlying meaning of a literary work. It is a message about
life, love, or human nature. It is not a cliche or a moral. The theme is often used to create a
thesis statement.
Foreshadowing: The use of hints or clues to suggest what will happen later in the story.
Imagery (also known as sensory details): language that appeals to the five senses.
Symbolism: the use of a person, place, or object to represent an abstract idea.
Conflict: a struggle between opposing forces.
Internal: within oneself
External: between two or more entities {this can include an individual or a society}
Plot Structure: the sequence of events that make up a story. The structure of a narrative is
generally as follows: Exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, and resolution.
Mood: the emotional atmosphere of the story.
Tone: expressed through the writer’s attitude or feelings about the subject of the story.
Close Reading: For scholars, “close reading” is a mode of analysis—one of many possible
modes, many of which can be used in conjunction with one another—that moves a reader
beyond comprehension of the text to interpretation of the text.
